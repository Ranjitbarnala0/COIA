PHILOSOPHY: EVERY SOLUTION IS AN INVENTION FROM THE SAME DNA
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The seed of COIA is: coherent optical interference IS computation.
Light fields multiply at the speed of physics. Accumulation is free (photons add).

v3.2 proved this seed works. But v3.2 solved its problems by borrowing:
  - Borrowed telecom AWGs for wavelength routing.
  - Borrowed electronic capacitors for accumulation.
  - Borrowed FPGA fabric for readout.
  - Borrowed heaters from thermo-optic tuning.
  - Borrowed PCIe electrical links for scaling.

Each borrowed piece created new problems. v4.0 solves every problem with MORE
coherence, not less. Every subsystem uses optical interference as its primitive.
When you need to fix something, you don't grab a wrench from a different toolbox —
you grow a new branch from the same root.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 1: MONOLITHIC PHOTONIC COMPUTE IC (PCIC)
             "Kill discrete optics entirely"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  8 discrete DFB lasers, external AWG, fiber-coupled 90° hybrids, separate
  photodetectors — each junction adds insertion loss (measured ~5 dB P95),
  alignment drift, and thermal coupling. The board has 50+ fiber connections
  that can fail. Thermal MPC fights coupling between adjacent components.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  A single silicon-nitride-on-insulator (SiN-OI) photonic integrated circuit,
  approximately 15×20 mm, that contains ALL optical compute elements:

  (a) HETEROGENEOUS III-V LASER INTEGRATION
      Instead of 8 external DFB lasers bonded to a PCB, grow InP gain sections
      directly on the SiN chip via micro-transfer-printing or wafer bonding.
      A single broad-gain III-V section feeds a SiN external-cavity structure
      with Vernier microring filters for wavelength selection. This gives you
      8 wavelengths from ONE gain medium sharing ONE thermal mass.

      Why this is inventive (not borrowed):
      Conventional photonics puts a laser array on a separate chip and couples
      via edge facets. Here, the laser IS the waveguide. The SiN cavity sets
      the linewidth (sub-100 kHz achievable with high-Q SiN rings, Q > 10^6).
      The III-V section only provides gain. Temperature tuning of the Vernier
      rings gives wavelength control without external components.

      Specifications:
        - Single III-V gain bar: 200 mW total, partitioned across 8 λ channels
        - LO tap: 20% of total → ~40 mW LO (>>3 mW per hybrid needed)
        - Signal channels: each ~20 mW post-modulation
        - Linewidth: ≤50 kHz (10 ms) via high-Q SiN cavity, improving on v3.2
        - RIN: ≤−160 dB/Hz (cavity filtering inherently suppresses RIN)
        - Wavelength grid: 100 GHz spacing, thermally tuned ±50 GHz

  (b) ON-CHIP AWG REPLACED BY ECHELLE GRATING OR CASCADED RING FILTERS
      The v3.2 AWG was a separate fiber-pigtailed component adding ~2 dB loss.
      On the PCIC, wavelength demux uses either:
        - SiN echelle grating (single etch, 0.3 dB loss, −30 dB crosstalk), or
        - Cascaded microring filters (each ring is a wavelength-selective switch
          that also serves as the first stage of optical accumulation — see
          Invention 3).
      Preference: cascaded rings, because they serve dual purpose.

  (c) ON-CHIP 90° HYBRIDS
      v3.2 used discrete 90° optical hybrids. On-chip, these are 4×4 MMI
      (multimode interference) couplers etched in SiN. Each is ~200×30 µm.
      For 8 wavelengths × 2 polarizations × I/Q = 32 hybrids, total area
      is ~0.2 mm² — negligible on a 15×20 mm die.

      Performance on-chip is BETTER than discrete:
        - Amplitude imbalance: ≤0.2 dB (lithographic precision, no fiber alignment)
        - Phase error: ≤0.5° (path lengths defined by mask, not assembly)
        - Insertion loss per hybrid: ≤0.3 dB

  (d) ON-CHIP BALANCED GERMANIUM PHOTODETECTORS
      Epitaxial Ge-on-Si photodetectors integrated at waveguide terminations.
      No wire bonds, no fiber coupling, no impedance discontinuities.
        - Responsivity: 0.8–1.0 A/W (improved coupling from evanescent integration)
        - Bandwidth: ≥40 GHz (short Ge section, low capacitance)
        - Cj: ≤60 fF (half of v3.2 discrete PDs)
        - Dark current: ≤1 nA @ 25°C
        - Saturation current: ≥20 mA

  (e) INSERTION LOSS BUDGET — CHIP TOTAL
      Source to detector, worst-case path:
        Laser to waveguide coupling:    0.5 dB  (heterogeneous, no facet)
        Wavelength demux (rings):       0.5 dB
        Polarization handling (on-chip): 0.3 dB
        90° hybrid (MMI):               0.3 dB
        Waveguide propagation (5 cm):   0.15 dB  (SiN ≤ 0.03 dB/cm)
        Total:                          1.75 dB

      Compare v3.2: 5.0 dB P95. This is a 3.25 dB improvement — which means
      2× the signal-to-noise ratio, or equivalently, half the accumulation time
      for the same precision. The chip ACCELERATES computation by reducing loss.

  FABRICATION:
    Standard SiN photonic foundry process (e.g., LIGENTEC AN800, or equivalent).
    200 mm or 300 mm wafer. SiN core 800 nm, SiO2 cladding.
    III-V micro-transfer-print for gain sections (post-processing).
    Ge epitaxy for photodetectors (standard process module).
    Metal heaters for Vernier ring tuning only (mW-scale, not the W-scale
    heaters of v3.2). These are part of the laser, not fighting thermal drift
    in the compute path — see Invention 2.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 2: COHERENT SELF-REFERENCING THERMAL IMMUNITY
             "Don't fight thermal drift — cancel it with coherence"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  Thermal drift causes phase errors in the interference. v3.2 fights this with
  per-zone heaters, a thermal transfer matrix H(T), regularized inversion,
  an inner optical PLL at 5 kHz, and an outer MPC loop at 100 ms. This is
  50+ heaters drawing watts, a complex control system, and 100 ms re-lock time.
  One thermal perturbation cascades across zones because the heaters couple.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  Use the coherent detection itself to reject thermal phase drift as common-mode
  noise. This is not a control system — it's a physical cancellation.

  HOW IT WORKS:
  On the PCIC, every signal path has a matched reference path of identical
  optical length, routed through the same thermal zone. The LO is split:
  half goes to the signal hybrid, half goes to the reference hybrid.

    Signal path:  LO_sig + E_signal → Hybrid_sig → PD_sig → I_sig + Q_sig
    Reference path: LO_ref + E_ref   → Hybrid_ref → PD_ref → I_ref + Q_ref

  Where E_ref is a known constant field (a tap from the LO itself, attenuated).

  Both paths experience the SAME thermal phase shift φ_thermal because they
  are physically adjacent on the same die (within ~100 µm). Balanced detection
  gives:

    I_sig = E_LO · E_signal · cos(Δφ + φ_thermal)
    I_ref = E_LO · E_ref    · cos(φ_thermal)

  From I_ref and Q_ref, you extract φ_thermal in real time. Then:

    I_corrected = I_sig · cos(φ_thermal) + Q_sig · sin(φ_thermal)

  This is a simple 2×2 rotation, done digitally after readout. No heaters.
  No PLL. No MPC. No re-lock time.

  WHY THIS IS INVENTIVE (NOT BORROWED):
  Common-mode rejection in balanced detectors is well known for intensity noise.
  But nobody uses a PARALLEL REFERENCE INTERFEROMETER on the same die to track
  and cancel thermal phase drift in an optical compute system. The invention is:
  the reference path computes NOTHING useful — it exists solely to measure what
  the thermal environment is doing, using the exact same physical mechanism
  (coherent interference) as the compute path. The sensor IS the compute
  primitive.

  SPECIFICATIONS:
    - Phase tracking bandwidth: DC to ~10 MHz (limited only by PD bandwidth)
    - Thermal rejection ratio: ≥40 dB for drift rates ≤1 K/s
    - Residual phase error: ≤0.1° RMS (vs 2.0° in v3.2)
    - Heater power: 0 W for compute paths (only mW for laser Vernier tuning)
    - Re-lock time: N/A — system never loses lock, it tracks continuously
    - Overhead: 1 reference channel per 8 signal channels (12.5% area overhead)

  WHAT THIS ELIMINATES FROM v3.2:
    - Section 8 (Thermal Identification and Control): DELETED ENTIRELY
    - Section 16 (Thermal Test Profiles): DELETED ENTIRELY
    - 50+ heater drivers and their power budget (~15 W): ELIMINATED
    - THERM register block (0x0800–0x08FF): ELIMINATED
    - OPLL register block: REDUCED to laser lock only
    - Calibration step P5 (thermal identification): ELIMINATED
    - Re-lock acceptance test: ELIMINATED


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 3: RESONANT OPTICAL ACCUMULATION (ROA)
             "Let photons do the accumulation — kill the capacitor"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  The VM (Vector-Matrix) mode integrates N=1024 symbol-rate photocurrents on a
  56 pF C0G capacitor. This requires: TIA → integrating capacitor → SAR ADC.
  The kT/C noise floor is 8.6 µV_rms. The integration is ELECTRICAL — photons
  are converted to electrons, then electrons are accumulated on a capacitor.
  This means every photon-to-electron conversion adds shot noise, and the TIA
  adds its own 4.5 pA/√Hz of input-referred noise. The capacitor leaks
  (R_leak ≥ 38 GΩ, but still finite). The state machine has 8 states.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  Accumulate the interference products OPTICALLY before photodetection.

  HOW IT WORKS:
  Replace the discrete TIA+capacitor with an on-chip high-Q microring resonator
  placed BEFORE the photodetector. The ring has quality factor Q and resonance
  linewidth δf = f_res / Q.

  Coherent light entering the ring at resonance circulates and builds up.
  After N round-trips, the intracavity field is the coherent sum of N input
  fields. This is optical accumulation — it's the same math as capacitor
  integration, but done with photon fields instead of electron currents.

  The effective accumulation number is:
    N_eff = (2 × Q) / (2π × n_g × L_ring × f_res / c) ≈ Q / (π × F)

  where F is the finesse. For a SiN ring with Q = 500,000 and FSR = 100 GHz:
    Finesse F = FSR / δf = 100 GHz / (193.5 THz / 500000) ≈ 258
    N_eff ≈ 500000 / (π × 258) ≈ 617

  For N_eff = 1024 (matching v3.2 default), we need Q ≈ 830,000 — achievable
  with state-of-the-art SiN (demonstrated Q > 10^7 in literature).

  The photodetector now sees the ACCUMULATED field, not individual symbols.
  One photodetection event replaces 1024 integrate-and-dump cycles.

  WHY THIS IS INVENTIVE (NOT BORROWED):
  High-Q rings are used in filters and lasers. Using them as ACCUMULATORS for
  optical compute products is new. The ring doesn't filter or route — it SUMS
  coherent fields over time. The resonance condition replaces the clock-gated
  integrate-and-dump state machine. The physics does the accumulation for free.

  WHAT THIS CHANGES:
    - TIA specification: RELAXED dramatically
      No longer integrating 15 GHz bandwidth signals.
      The ring outputs a quasi-DC field (bandwidth = δf ≈ 400 kHz for Q=500k).
      TIA becomes a simple low-bandwidth (1 MHz) transimpedance stage.
      Input-referred noise requirement drops from 4.5 pA/√Hz to ~0.1 pA/√Hz
      (easily met by any low-noise op-amp at this bandwidth).

    - ADC specification: RELAXED
      No longer 12-bit SAR at 10–20 MS/s per channel.
      Now: 16-bit sigma-delta ADC at 1 MS/s — higher resolution, lower power,
      commodity parts. Or integrate ADC on the electronic die.

    - Noise budget: IMPROVED
      Optical accumulation adds fields COHERENTLY (amplitudes add).
      Electrical accumulation adds INCOHERENTLY (powers add, noise averages √N).
      Coherent accumulation gives N× signal vs √N× noise = √N SNR improvement
      over electrical accumulation of same N. For N=1024, this is 30 dB
      vs the 15 dB you'd get electrically. Effectively doubles the ENOB.

    - State machine: ELIMINATED
      No RESET→PRECHARGE→INTEGRATE→HOLD→TRANSFER→CONVERT→DUMP→IDLE.
      The ring charges at its natural rate, reaches steady-state, and the PD
      continuously outputs the accumulated result. Readout is asynchronous.

  SPECIFICATIONS:
    Ring radius: ~200 µm (FSR ≈ 100 GHz)
    Q factor: 800,000–1,000,000 (SiN-OI, demonstrated)
    Effective N: 1024–1300
    Accumulation bandwidth: ~400 kHz (self-determined by Q)
    Readout rate: up to 400 kS/s per channel (Nyquist of ring bandwidth)
    Ring coupling: critically coupled (maximizes extinction and buildup)
    Tuning: thermal micro-heater on ring (~2 mW per ring to trim resonance)
      — NOTE: these are PER-RING µW-scale trims, fundamentally different from
        the v3.2 zone heaters. They tune the ACCUMULATOR, not fight drift.

  WHAT THIS ELIMINATES FROM v3.2:
    - Section 5 (TIA Specification): REPLACED with trivial low-BW amplifier
    - Section 6.1 (VM/CDSR): REPLACED entirely by ring resonator physics
    - CDSR register block: ELIMINATED (no state machine to control)
    - kT/C noise floor: IRRELEVANT (no capacitor)
    - SAR ADC trimming and linearity cal: ELIMINATED

INVENTION 4: PROGRAMMABLE COHERENT MESH (PCM)
             "The routing IS the computation"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  Fixed 8-channel AWG routes wavelengths to fixed 90° hybrids. The matrix
  operation is hardwired. To change the computation, you must change the
  optical input (modulate different weights onto different wavelengths).
  This puts all the programmability burden on the electronic side (modulators,
  DACs) and makes the optical path a fixed pipeline.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  Replace the AWG + fixed hybrids with a PROGRAMMABLE unitary mesh of tunable
  Mach-Zehnder Interferometers (MZIs). This is a Reck/Clements decomposition
  of an arbitrary N×N unitary matrix into a triangular array of 2×2 MZI cells.

  For N=16 inputs (8 wavelengths × 2 polarizations):
    Number of MZI cells: N(N−1)/2 = 120
    Each MZI has 2 phase shifters (θ, φ) → 240 tunable phases
    Total mesh area: ~8 mm × 4 mm on SiN (each MZI is ~500 µm long)

  The mesh performs SIMULTANEOUSLY:
    (a) Wavelength demultiplexing (learned, not fixed geometry)
    (b) Polarization handling (learned, not fixed splitter/rotator)
    (c) The matrix-vector multiply (the actual computation)
    (d) Coherent combining of partial products

  All in a SINGLE pass of light through the mesh. No separate AWG, no separate
  hybrids, no separate combiners. One optical fabric does everything.

  WHY THIS IS INVENTIVE (NOT BORROWED):
  Programmable photonic meshes exist in research. But they're used for
  COMMUNICATION (switching) or QUANTUM computing (unitary transforms on
  single photons). Using a Clements mesh as a FUSED demux + compute + combine
  engine for classical coherent interference MAC operations — where the mesh
  simultaneously separates wavelengths AND performs the matrix operation —
  is a new application of the principle. The mesh learns its own demux
  function through calibration (Invention 7), eliminating the need for
  precision-fabricated AWG geometry.

  PROGRAMMABILITY:
    Each phase shifter: thermo-optic (SiN) or electro-optic (if using Si hybrid)
      - Thermo-optic: ~25 mW per π shift, 10 µs response, 10-bit DAC control
      - Electro-optic (piezo-actuated SiN): ~10 µW per π shift, 1 ns response
        [PREFERRED — eliminates thermal cross-talk within the mesh itself]

    For 240 phase shifters with piezo actuation: total mesh tuning power < 2.5 mW
    Compare v3.2 AWG: passive (0 W) but fixed and lossy (2 dB).
    The 2.5 mW is negligible, and the mesh is PROGRAMMABLE + lower loss.

  SCALING:
    N=16: 120 MZIs → 32 mm² — fits on PCIC
    N=32: 496 MZIs → 130 mm² — fits on PCIC with some reorg
    N=64: 2016 MZIs → 520 mm² — needs tiled PCIC (see Invention 6)
    N=128: 8128 MZIs → ~2100 mm² — wafer-scale photonics or multi-chiplet

  The mesh topology naturally scales quadratically — but so does the compute
  capability. An N×N mesh performs an N×N matrix-vector multiply in ONE optical
  pass. There's no electrical equivalent that does a full 128×128 multiply in
  a single clock cycle at this power.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 5: PHOTONIC-ELECTRONIC CO-PACKAGE (PECP)
             "The electronic die is a readout ASIC, not a computer"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  The FPGA/SoC draws up to 45 A at 0.85 V = ~38 W just for the digital core.
  Total board power is 250 W. The FPGA does: high-speed ADC interfacing,
  DMA, state machine control, thermal MPC, and PCIe endpoint. Most of this
  power goes to tasks that SHOULDN'T EXIST if the optical path did its job.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  Co-package a tiny custom ASIC (the "Readout and Control Die" — RCD) with the
  PCIC on a silicon or glass interposer, connected via micro-bump arrays.

  The RCD does ONLY:
    (a) ADC readout of accumulated signals (16-bit ΣΔ, 1 MS/s × 256 channels)
    (b) DAC drive for mesh phase shifters (10-bit, 240 channels, static)
    (c) DAC drive for ring resonator trims (10-bit, 256 channels, quasi-static)
    (d) Reference channel processing (φ_thermal extraction, 2×2 rotation)
    (e) PCIe Gen5/CXL 3.0 endpoint (host interface)
    (f) Health monitoring (power, temperature sensors, LO lock detect)

  WHAT THE RCD DOES NOT DO:
    - No high-speed (GHz) signal processing
    - No TIA (ring accumulation eliminated the need)
    - No SAR ADC at high speed (ΣΔ at 1 MS/s replaces 250 MS/s SAR)
    - No state machine for integrate-and-dump (ring physics replaces it)
    - No thermal MPC (coherent self-referencing replaces it)
    - No FPGA fabric (everything is hardened logic, no reconfigurability waste)

  RCD SPECIFICATIONS:
    Process: 12 nm or 16 nm FinFET (no need for bleeding edge)
    Die size: ~25 mm² (compare: the v3.2 FPGA was probably 200+ mm²)
    Core voltage: 0.8 V
    Total die power: ≤8 W
    I/O: 256 ADC channels + 500 DAC channels + PCIe Gen5 x8 (x16 optional)
    Package: 2.5D with PCIC on silicon interposer, 45 µm micro-bump pitch

  CO-PACKAGE ASSEMBLY:
    Silicon interposer: ~30×25 mm
    PCIC die: 15×20 mm, flip-chip bonded to interposer (optical face up)
    RCD die: 5×5 mm, flip-chip bonded adjacent to PCIC
    Micro-bumps: RCD↔interposer↔PCIC for all analog signals (short, low-noise)
    Optical I/O: Grating couplers or edge couplers on PCIC for fiber attach
    Thermal: PCIC backside contacts copper lid; RCD has separate thermal path

  POWER BUDGET — COMPLETE MODULE:
    III-V laser gain section:         1.5 W
    Mesh phase shifters (piezo):      0.003 W  (2.5 mW)
    Ring resonator trims:             0.5 W    (256 rings × 2 mW average)
    Readout/Control Die (RCD):        8.0 W
    Reference channel processing:     0.2 W    (within RCD)
    Voltage regulators (overhead):    2.0 W
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    TOTAL MODULE POWER:              ~12 W

    Compare v3.2: 250 W (typical), 260 W (with ERM-64).
    This is a 20× power reduction.

    Even if we're conservative and double everything for margin: ~25 W.
    Still 10× better than v3.2, and 10× better J/op than H100.


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 6: COHERENT OPTICAL FABRIC (COF)
             "Scaling without breaking coherence"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  Section 9 specifies incoherent WDM for inter-card communication, 400G
  Ethernet PHY, FEC, CXL memory — all ELECTRICAL at the card boundary.
  Every time you cross a card boundary, you lose the coherent advantage.
  The signal goes from light → electrons → processing → electrons → light.
  Each conversion adds latency, power, and noise.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  Maintain optical coherence across the entire rack by distributing a shared
  LO from a single ultra-stable source to every module.

  ARCHITECTURE:
    One rack contains:
      - 1 Master LO Unit (MLU): ultra-low-noise laser, linewidth <10 kHz,
        output 1 W, distributed via PM fiber tree to all modules
      - Up to 64 PECP modules on OAM-style carriers
      - Coherent optical backplane: each module has grating couplers that
        connect to a passive SiN routing PIC in the backplane
      - The backplane PIC is itself a large Clements mesh — it routes
        intermediate coherent results between modules WITHOUT photodetection

  INTER-MODULE COHERENT LINK:
    Because all modules share the same LO phase reference, a coherent signal
    from Module A arrives at Module B with a KNOWN phase relationship. Module B
    can interfere this signal with its own LO and extract the result directly.
    No ADC, no DAC, no FEC, no Ethernet PHY.

    Latency per hop: ~5 ns (propagation through backplane PIC)
    Compare v3.2: 2 µs per hop (99%ile, including FEC/PHY/DMA)
    This is 400× lower latency.

    Bandwidth per link: limited only by WDM channel count × symbol rate
    With 64 wavelengths at 100 GHz spacing over C-band: ~6.4 Tb/s coherent
    Compare v3.2: 400 Gb/s Ethernet per link

  WHY THIS IS INVENTIVE (NOT BORROWED):
    Coherent optical interconnect exists in telecom. But telecom re-detects
    and re-generates at every node. Here, the signal stays coherent END TO END
    across the entire rack. Module B doesn't "receive" data — it CONTINUES
    the interference computation that Module A started. The rack is one
    distributed interferometer, not a network of independent computers
    talking over a wire.

  MEMORY ACCESS:
    CXL 3.0 memory pool connects via coherent links (not electrical CXL).
    Memory controllers have their own small PCIC for coherent I/O.
    Effective memory bandwidth per module: ≥200 GB/s (coherent, no FEC overhead)
    Pool size: ≥40 TB (same as v3.2 target, but accessed coherently)

  WHAT THIS ELIMINATES FROM v3.2:
    - Section 9 (Interconnect, Fabric, Memory): REPLACED entirely
    - 400G Ethernet PHY: ELIMINATED
    - FEC RS(544,514): ELIMINATED (coherent link doesn't need FEC at these BERs)
    - 3-stage Clos topology: REPLACED by programmable backplane mesh


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INVENTION 7: SELF-CALIBRATING PILOT PROTOCOL (SCPP)
             "The system calibrates itself using its own computation"
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

v3.2 PROBLEM:
  Calibration requires external equipment (precision current sources, optical
  tones, VNA S21 sweeps), factory procedures (TDC_Calibrate, VM_Linearity,
  SM_Trim), stored NVM coefficients, and re-calibration on thermal events.
  Sections 13, 15, 16 are dedicated to calibration and test.

v4.0 INVENTION:
  The PCIC can measure its own transfer matrix using pilot tones injected
  through the mesh, detected by its own photodetectors, and corrected by
  its own RCD.

  HOW IT WORKS:
  1. Inject known phase/amplitude patterns into the mesh inputs
     (using the on-chip laser + on-chip modulators at known DAC settings)
  2. Measure the output at each photodetector channel
  3. The measured outputs = M × inputs, where M is the actual transfer matrix
  4. Since inputs are known, solve for M
  5. Compare M to the desired matrix; compute correction DAC settings
  6. Iterate until ||M_actual − M_desired|| < ε

  This runs continuously in background during idle cycles, and as a fast
  sweep during boot. No external equipment. No factory calibration. No NVM
  coefficient tables that go stale.

  CALIBRATION TIME:
    Boot self-cal: ~50 ms (one full matrix sweep + correction)
    Background tracking: continuous, ~1 correction/second
    Compare v3.2: bring-up checklist has 12 manual steps, hours of procedure

  WHY THIS IS INVENTIVE:
    The system uses coherent interference to MEASURE coherent interference
    errors. The calibration primitive IS the compute primitive. There's no
    separate sensor, no separate reference standard. The pilot protocol is
    just a specific computation that the system already knows how to do —
    multiplying a known vector by the mesh and checking the answer.

  WHAT THIS ELIMINATES FROM v3.2:
    - Section 13 (Manufacturing Test): REDUCED to simple power-on self-test
    - Section 15 (Calibration Scripts): REPLACED by on-chip SCPP
    - Section 20 (Bring-up Checklist): REDUCED from 12 steps to 3:
        1. Power on → self-cal → verify (automated, ~1 minute)
        2. Host enumeration (PCIe)
        3. Run acceptance benchmark (automated)
    - External calibration equipment: ELIMINATED from BOM

  Zone A (0–5 mm): Laser gain section + Vernier ring cavities (8 channels)
  Zone B (5–8 mm): Ring-based wavelength demux / accumulator array
  Zone C (8–16 mm): Programmable Clements mesh (16×16 = 120 MZI cells)
  Zone D (16–18 mm): Reference interferometer array (32 channels)
  Zone E (18–20 mm): Photodetector array + electrical pad ring for micro-bumps

1.4 OPTICAL I/O
  Edge couplers (preferred) or grating couplers for fiber attach.
  LO input: 1 PM fiber, 40 mW.
  Signal inputs: 8 SM fibers or 1 fiber array (8-channel).
  Inter-module coherent output: 8 SM fibers to backplane.
  Coupling loss per facet: ≤1.0 dB (edge), ≤1.5 dB (grating).

2) RCD (READOUT AND CONTROL DIE) SPECIFICATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2.1 PROCESS AND DIE
  TSMC 16FFC or GlobalFoundries 12LP; die ≤25 mm².
  Core voltage: 0.8 V; I/O voltage: 1.8 V.

2.2 ADC SUBSYSTEM
  256 channels of 16-bit sigma-delta ADC.
  Sample rate: 1 MS/s per channel (oversampled; ring bandwidth ~400 kHz).
  Input range: ±1.0 V differential.
  ENOB: ≥14.0 bits (thermal noise limited at this bandwidth).
  Total ADC power: ~2 W (256 × ~8 mW per channel).

2.3 DAC SUBSYSTEM — MESH PROGRAMMING
  240 channels of 10-bit voltage-output DAC for MZI phase shifters.
  Update rate: quasi-static (only changes when reprogramming the matrix).
  Output: 0–3.3 V (piezo drive) or 0–1.0 V (thermo-optic fallback).
  Total DAC power: ~0.5 W.

2.4 DAC SUBSYSTEM — RING TRIMS
  256 channels of 10-bit current-output DAC for ring resonator micro-heaters.
  Output: 0–5 mA (2 mW typical per ring).
  Total DAC power: ~0.3 W.

2.5 REFERENCE PROCESSING UNIT (RPU)
  Hardened logic block that:
    - Reads 32 reference channel ADCs continuously
    - Extracts φ_thermal per zone (CORDIC-based arctangent, 16-bit)
    - Computes 2×2 rotation corrections for adjacent signal channels
    - Applies corrections in fixed-point before output to host
  Latency: ≤1 µs from ADC sample to corrected output.
  Power: ~0.2 W.

2.6 SELF-CALIBRATION ENGINE (SCE)
  Hardened logic block that:
    - Generates pilot patterns (known phase/amplitude vectors)
    - Drives them into mesh via DAC subsystem
    - Reads back results via ADC subsystem
    - Computes transfer matrix M_actual (fixed-point matrix multiply)
    - Computes correction Δθ for each MZI phase shifter
    - Updates mesh DACs
  Boot cal: ~50 ms. Background cal: 1 iteration/s, ~100 µW incremental power.

2.7 HOST INTERFACE
  PCIe Gen5 x8 endpoint (upgradeable to x16 or CXL 3.0 via optional PHY).
  DMA engine with scatter-gather; 4 rings, 64 MSI-X vectors.
  Effective payload bandwidth: ≥24 GB/s (x8), ≥48 GB/s (x16).
  BAR0: MMIO registers (compatible superset of v3.2 map, consolidated).
  BAR1: Doorbell page for DMA submission.

2.8 HEALTH AND TELEMETRY
  On-die temperature sensors: 4 (corners), ±0.5°C.
  PCIC temperature sensors: via reference channels (more accurate than RTDs).
  Power monitors: on-chip current sense for each rail.
  Telemetry bus: internal, polled at 10 Hz, logged to host via MSI-X.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5) CLOCKING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5.1 ON-MODULE
  No OCXO required. No 15 GHz PLL required.
  The optical symbol rate is set by the LASER MODULATION, not an electronic
  clock. The RCD needs only a standard 100 MHz crystal oscillator for its
  digital logic and ADC timing.

  Crystal: 100 MHz; jitter ≤500 fs_rms (trivial spec); 1.8V; 3×2.5 mm.

  WHY THE 15 GHz CLOCK IS GONE:
  In v3.2, the 15 GHz clock drove the symbol-rate sampling and the TDC for
  metastability checking. With resonant optical accumulation (Invention 3),
  there is no symbol-rate electronic sampling. The ring accumulates optically
  and the readout is at ~1 MS/s. The TDC is eliminated because there's no
  high-speed decision boundary to guard.

5.2 RACK-LEVEL
  Master LO Unit provides optical frequency reference (not electronic clock).
  Phase coherence across modules is maintained OPTICALLY, not electronically.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7) FIRMWARE: BOOT AND RUNTIME (SIMPLIFIED)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.1 BOOT SEQUENCE
  S0_POWER_ON:    Rails sequence; RCD resets; BIST runs (10 ms)
  S1_LASER_LOCK:  Enable III-V bias; Vernier rings tune to grid (20 ms)
  S2_SELF_CAL:    Run SCPP; program mesh + rings; verify (50 ms)
  S3_READY:       Assert READY; enumerate on PCIe; accept host commands

  Total boot time: ~80 ms (cold) / ~50 ms (warm, laser already locked)
  Compare v3.2: 12-step manual bring-up procedure

7.2 RUNTIME STATES
  R0_IDLE:    Module powered, mesh programmed, waiting for host command
  R1_RUN:     Continuous ADC readout → DMA to host
  R2_RECAL:   Background self-cal (transparent to host, ~1% throughput loss)
  R3_REPROGRAM: Host writing new matrix → mesh DAC update → verify → R1_RUN
  R4_FAULT:   Persistent error; module reports to host, awaits reset

7.3 HOST API (SIMPLIFIED)
  The host driver sees a simple interface:
    1. Write matrix weights to MESH_PHASE registers (or upload via DMA)
    2. Write CTRL_CMD = RUN
    3. Read results from DMA ring buffers (interrupt-driven or polled)
    4. Write new matrix when needed → CTRL_CMD = REPROGRAM → RUN

  No calibration scripts. No thermal management. No TDC tuning.
  The firmware handles everything internally via SCPP and RPU.

8) COHERENT OPTICAL BACKPLANE (RACK-LEVEL)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8.1 MASTER LO UNIT (MLU)
  Ultra-low-noise external-cavity laser.
  Output: 1 W CW at 1550 nm; linewidth ≤10 kHz.
  Distribution: 1×64 PM fiber splitter tree to module slots.
  Per-module LO power: ~15 mW (after splitter + connector losses).
  MLU power: ~5 W (separate from module power budget).
  Redundancy: hot-standby MLU with automatic failover (fiber switch).

8.2 BACKPLANE PIC
  Passive SiN routing chip on the backplane; 50×80 mm.
  Implements a configurable 64×64 optical switch matrix.
  Insertion loss: ≤3 dB any path.
  Reconfiguration time: ~10 µs (thermo-optic switches).
  No active electronics on the backplane PIC — purely passive waveguides
  with thermo-optic phase shifters driven by a backplane controller.

8.3 RACK SPECIFICATION
  Modules per rack: up to 64
  Rack power (compute): 64 × 25 W = 1600 W (vs 64 × 260 W = 16,640 W for v3.2)
  Rack power (MLU + cooling + backplane): ~400 W
  Total rack power: ~2000 W for 64 modules
  Rack compute density: 64 × (16×16 mesh × 1024 accumulation × 400 kS/s)
                      = ~2.7 × 10^12 effective MAC/s per rack
  Energy efficiency: ~2000 W / 2.7 TMAC/s ≈ 0.74 pJ/MAC

  For comparison, H100 at 300W achieves ~1000 TFLOPS peak (FP8) = 3.3 pJ/FLOP.
  At equivalent precision (FP16): H100 ~500 TFLOPS = 6.0 pJ/FLOP.
  COIA v4.0 at 14+ effective bits: 0.74 pJ/MAC. This is ~8× better J/op.

PART III — THE INVENTION PRINCIPLE, FORMALIZED
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

For the record, here is the principle that guided every decision in this document:

  COIA INVENTION PRINCIPLE:
  "When a problem arises in a coherent optical interference system,
   the solution must use coherent optical interference as its primitive.
   If a solution borrows from a different domain (electronic control,
   mechanical alignment, thermal brute-force), it will create secondary
   problems that require further borrowed solutions, until the original
   invention is buried. Every fix must grow from the same root."

  Applied:
    Problem: Thermal drift.        Solution: Coherent self-referencing.
    Problem: Accumulation noise.   Solution: Resonant optical accumulation.
    Problem: Fixed routing.        Solution: Programmable coherent mesh.
    Problem: Inter-card scaling.   Solution: Coherent optical backplane.
    Problem: Calibration drift.    Solution: Coherent self-calibration.
    Problem: Complex electronics.  Solution: Let optics do the work; readout only.
    Problem: Discrete component loss. Solution: Monolithic photonic IC.

  In every case: MORE coherence, not less. Every invention is a branch
  from the same root.
